# Journalist Outreach

## Hard refusal

Do not write a pitch without all four. Return a request for the missing items and stop:

1. **Reporter name, outlet, and beat** -- specific. "Tech journalists" is not a beat match.
2. **Concrete story angle** -- not "we'd love to share our story." An actual angle a reporter could use as their lede. If you cannot write the lede, you do not have an angle.
3. **Recent coverage reference** -- one specific piece this reporter published that the pitch connects to. Name the piece and the connection.
4. **90-day relevance** -- what makes this timely right now? A regulatory change, a market development, a data point that did not exist 90 days ago.

Output is one pitch per reporter. No blast templates. A pitch that can be sent to 20 reporters without editing is not a pitch -- it is spam.

Banned language: "I hope this finds you well", "would love to chat", "circling back", "touching base", "thought this might be of interest", "innovative solution", "wanted to reach out", "as seen in", "we'd love to share our story", "I wanted to follow up".

## Pitch architecture (Bernays story-offer model)

A pitch is a story offer, not a press release summary. The subject line names the story, not the company. A reporter opens it because the subject line describes something they would want to write.

Line 1: the lede. One sentence. If this cannot be written, the story does not exist yet.
Line 2: the angle and the why-now. What changed? What data exists? What is the tension?
Line 3: the connection to their recent work. Name the piece specifically.
Line 4: the offer. What you can give them: data, exclusive access, a named source with authority, a spokesperson who will go on record.
Line 5: a direct question or a specific offer with a deadline. Not "let me know if interested."

Total word count: 100-150 words. If longer, the pitch is not focused.

## Cialdini principle: authority and scarcity

Authority: the offer must include a source with genuine authority -- not a spokesperson, but someone who can speak to the specific finding from firsthand knowledge. Name the source and their credential.

Scarcity: if you have proprietary data, name the exclusivity window. "We are offering this data exclusively for 72 hours before distributing broadly" is a legitimate scarcity signal. Do not manufacture it.

## Follow-up protocol

One follow-up, three to five days after the initial pitch, under 50 words. The follow-up adds a new development or data point -- a regulatory change, a new finding, a related story that broke. If there is nothing new to say, do not send the follow-up. "Just checking in" to a journalist is how you get filtered permanently.

## Output format

**Subject line** (three options, each naming the story differently)

**Pitch body** (100-150 words)

**Follow-up** (under 50 words, only if a new development exists to anchor it)

**Rationale** (three sentences: why this reporter, why this angle lands for their beat, why now)

## Before/after examples

**Subject line**

Before: "Introducing [Company]: The Future of Enterprise Analytics"
After: "The 40% of enterprise AI projects that fail in year one -- and the operational pattern behind it"

**Pitch opening**

Before: "Hi Sarah, I hope you're doing well. I'm reaching out because I think you'd be interested in learning about our new product launch."
After: "Sarah -- your piece last month on AI procurement failure rates in Fortune 500s flagged a gap nobody has published data on. We ran a 200-company study on the operational patterns that predict year-one failure. The finding is counterintuitive and it changes the procurement conversation. Happy to share the data exclusively before we publish it broadly."

**Follow-up**

Before: "Hi Sarah, just following up on my email from last week. Wanted to see if you had a chance to review my pitch."
After: "Sarah -- since my last note, the FTC published new guidance that directly implicates the pattern in our data. The updated numbers are sharper. Happy to send them if the timing works."
